# Knee Valgus ("Knee Cave") During the Squat

## What it is
Knee valgus is medial collapse of the knee during the squat — the femur
adducts and internally rotates so the knee tracks inside the line of the
foot rather than over the toes. It is most visible at the bottom of the
descent and during the initial drive out of the hole.

## Why it happens
Common contributors, in rough order of how often they show up in
intermediate lifters:

1. **Insufficient hip abductor activation.** The gluteus medius and the
   posterior fibers of the gluteus maximus stabilize the femur in the
   frontal plane. Under-recruitment lets the femur collapse inward.
2. **Stance too narrow for the lifter's hip structure.** Lifters with
   wider hip sockets or longer femurs often need a wider stance and more
   external foot rotation than the population default.
3. **Limited ankle dorsiflexion.** Restricted ankle ROM forces a more
   upright torso and shifts compensation upstream. The knees may dive
   forward and inward to keep the bar over midfoot.
4. **Heavy load relative to current strength.** Knee valgus often appears
   as a fatigue-driven failure mode on the last rep of a hard set.

## Risk
Mild, transient valgus that the lifter can correct on the next rep is
not strongly associated with injury. Repeated, large-amplitude valgus
under heavy load is associated with patellofemoral pain and ACL strain
in the literature. Severity, rep count under load, and lifter history
matter more than the presence of any valgus on a single rep.

## Cues that work
Cue selection is highly individual; bias toward the lifter's stated
preference (see `cue-selection-internal-vs-external.md`).

External (attentional focus on environment):
- "Spread the floor with your feet."
- "Push the floor apart."
- "Screw your feet into the ground."

Internal (attentional focus on body):
- "Drive your knees out."
- "Fire your glutes wide."
- "Tension through the hips."
