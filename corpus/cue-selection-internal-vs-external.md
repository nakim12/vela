# Cue Selection: Internal vs External Focus

## The two categories
- **External cue.** Attentional focus on the *environment* or the
  *outcome*. Examples: "spread the floor," "push the floor away,"
  "drive the bar through the ceiling."
- **Internal cue.** Attentional focus on a *body part* or *muscle*.
  Examples: "squeeze your glutes," "brace your ribs down," "drive your
  knees out."

## What the evidence generally favors
Across most performance tasks, external focus tends to produce better
*movement outcomes* — bar speed, jump height, shot accuracy — than
internal focus. This is the constrained-action hypothesis associated
with Gabriele Wulf and collaborators in the motor-learning literature.

## When internal cues win
Despite the general pattern, internal cues outperform external cues for
some lifters and some tasks:

- **Stiffness/bracing tasks** (the brace, the lockout) often respond
  better to internal cues that tension a specific structure.
- **Beginners** who lack the proprioceptive map needed to translate an
  abstract external instruction into the right joint motion.
- **Lifters with a stated preference** for internal cues. Self-reported
  preference is itself a strong moderator. If the lifter reports
  "internal cues click for me," respect it.

## Practical rules for the in-set cue
1. Default to external for movement-shape failures (knee cave, bar path
   drift, depth).
2. Switch to internal for bracing failures (forward dump on a lifter
   with a back history, mid-set lordotic collapse).
3. Always honor a lifter's stated cue-preference memory over the
   population default.
4. Voice cues during a set are 3–8 words. Save explanations for between
   sets.
