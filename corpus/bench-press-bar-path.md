# Bench Press: Bar Path, Elbow Position, and Shoulder Safety

## Why bar path matters
The barbell on a bench press doesn't travel straight up. A safe, strong
press follows a shallow J-curve: the bar starts above the shoulders,
descends to a point on the lower chest / upper abdomen, and presses back
up over the shoulders at lockout. A vertical-only path forces the elbows
to flare 90 degrees from the torso, which is the position most strongly
associated with anterior shoulder pain and pec/biceps tendon strain.

## Two failure modes the rules engine flags

### BAR_PATH_DRIFT (forward drift at lockout)
The bar tracks toward the lifter's face on the way up, ending closer to
the eyes than the shoulders. Causes:

- **Triceps weakness** — once the bar passes the sticking point the lifter
  recruits the front delts to "muscle through," pulling the bar forward.
- **Elbow flare too aggressive** — wide elbow position torques the bar
  toward the head as the press completes.
- **Setup drift** — the lifter starts with the bar already too far over
  the face (under-cued shoulder retraction).

External cues that work: "press the bar back, not up," "drive your head
into the bench." Internal cue if the lifter prefers them: "squeeze the
triceps long."

### ELBOW_FLARE (elbows >75 deg from torso at the bottom)
At the bottom of the rep the upper arm is roughly perpendicular to the
torso instead of tucked toward 45-65 deg. Risks:

- Anterior glenohumeral stress — the humeral head loads the front of the
  capsule.
- Pec tear risk on the eccentric, especially under heavy load with paused
  reps.

The fix is almost always the touch point, not the arms directly. If the
bar touches lower (sternum, not collarbone) the elbows naturally tuck.
Cues: "touch the bottom of your sternum," "tuck your elbows toward your
hips on the way down."

## Anthropometry caveat
Lifters with **long arms** have a longer bar path and a harder lockout —
the bar travels further. They benefit from a wider grip (within IPF
legal limits) and more aggressive arch. Lifters with **short arms /
broad chest** have a shorter path and often press more naturally with a
narrower grip and tucked elbows. Apply the elbow-flare rule with this
context: a 70-deg elbow on a long-armed lifter may be optimal; the same
angle on a short-armed lifter is probably loose.
