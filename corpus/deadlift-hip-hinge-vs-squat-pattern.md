# Deadlift: Hip Hinge vs Squat Pattern

## The two motor patterns
A correct conventional deadlift is a **hip hinge** — the hips travel
back and the bar stays vertical over the mid-foot, with the knees
unlocking just enough to clear the bar. A common error is treating the
deadlift as a **squat** — the hips drop down and forward as if starting
a squat, so the bar starts further from the body and the lifter has to
swing it backward into position. The squat-pattern deadlift is less
mechanically efficient and dramatically increases lumbar shear stress.

## Failure modes the rules engine flags

### HIPS_RISE_TOO_FAST
Off the floor the hips shoot up and the bar drags along the shins or
loses contact with them, leaving the lifter with the bar on stiff legs
and a horizontal back. Causes:

- **Quad weakness off the floor** — the lifter can't hold a strong hip
  angle, so the hips rise to convert the lift into a stiff-leg.
- **Set-up too low** — the lifter started in a squat position the legs
  can't actually drive out of.
- **Cue confusion** — coaches who say "push the floor away" can produce
  this if the lifter substitutes pushing for hip extension.

Cues that work: "wedge yourself between the bar and the floor," "push
the floor away *and* drive your hips through" (combined cue), "shoulders
and hips rise together."

### LUMBAR_FLEXION (back rounds at the start)
The lower back loses neutral curve before the bar leaves the floor. This
is the highest-injury-risk failure mode in the deadlift catalog. Causes:

- **Insufficient bracing** — the lifter is not creating intra-abdominal
  pressure before initiating the pull.
- **Bar started too far away** — forces the lower back to absorb the
  lever-arm change.
- **Hamstring inflexibility** — the lifter cannot reach a neutral spine
  in the start position to begin with.

Safety cue: the rules engine should bias toward a STOP cue if lumbar
flexion appears under load (severity high). Personalized accommodation
(e.g. cleared by PT for a known small flexion at lockout) is one of the
few cases where threshold override is reasonable.

## Anthropometry caveat
**Long-armed, short-torso** lifters have a much shorter range of motion
and naturally start with a more vertical torso. They can look almost
upright at lockout with low risk. **Short-armed, long-torso** lifters
have a longer range and a more horizontal start position even when
performed correctly — don't confuse a long lever arm for poor form.

For the latter group the *sumo* deadlift (wider stance, hands inside
knees) significantly shortens the bar path and reduces lumbar shear; if
the lifter has a sumo-friendly hip structure (open externally rotated
hip range), it's a legitimate technique choice rather than "cheating."
