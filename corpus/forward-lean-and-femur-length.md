# Forward Lean, Torso Angle, and Femur Length in the Squat

## The geometric reality
Squat torso angle is largely determined by the lifter's anthropometry,
not by cue selection. Two femur-related factors dominate:

- **Femur length relative to torso length.** Longer femurs require a
  more forward torso lean to keep the bar over midfoot. A lifter with a
  femur:torso ratio above ~1.0 will look meaningfully more "bent over"
  at the bottom than a lifter with a ratio of 0.85, even with identical
  technique and identical stance.
- **Hip socket geometry.** Lifters with deeper, more anteriorly-oriented
  hip sockets reach end-range hip flexion sooner and tip the pelvis
  forward earlier in the descent.

A coach who applies the same "chest up" target to every lifter is
fighting their skeleton.

## When forward lean is a problem
"Forward dump" — a sudden, reactive forward pitch out of the hole —
is different from a lifter's natural torso angle:

- **Natural lean.** Smooth, consistent across reps, scales with depth.
- **Forward dump.** Sharp shift mid-rep, often paired with knees
  shooting forward, hips rising faster than the chest, or the bar
  drifting off the back. This is a strength or technique issue, not an
  anthropometry issue.

Forward dump on a lifter with a known low-back history is a stop signal,
not a cue opportunity.

## Cues that target forward dump (not natural lean)
- "Brace your ribs down" (internal — bias toward this for lifters who
  already have a stiff midline).
- "Push your back into the bar."
- "Hips and chest rise together."

For natural lean, the appropriate response is usually no cue at all
during the set, and a between-set check that the bar path is staying
over midfoot.
